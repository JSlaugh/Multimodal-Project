Introduction
Data mining, data warehouses, data privacy, and big data— all these words have sprung up over the last 20 years as computers have become more sophisticated. The public jokes about how big brother is always watching, your phone is always listening, and our phones know increasingly more about us than our families. Are these jokes far from the truth? In today’s world it’s not unlikely.
For example, one might look up the MCAT on google and then be bombarded with ads about study tips for the following weeks (Aragon). One search for a wedding ring might lead to a social media feed full of overpriced bands. One click comparing cat food prices leads to ads for pet flea remover. How does this happen? Cookies play a big part in it. Not the sugary ones that kids love, but the ones that companies keep on consumer’s computers. This paper will discuss data mining, big data, and several companies that are involved in this industry— informing readers on the growing stockpiles of their data and what it is currently being used for. In addition, I will focus on where the technology world is headed and what might potentially change to protect the public and help their lives to remain private.
Browsing Data Collection
Now consumers might wonder how data is collected. It all starts when they click on a new website and are then prompted to accept the terms and services. Most users click “yes” and move on with their day. Without realizing it they have agreed for the computer to download another cookie which will track their data. Some might wonder why this is allowed. Cookies are a means for the website to be personalized to a consumer’s tastes and needs. They access previous internet searches and create a website tailored to a specific individual (Computer). The problem lies with different types of cookies. Some cookies only last for the duration of your stay on a website, but some can last longer, even up to several years.
These cookies can be used to make an internet profile for you, which can be sold between companies. For example, Target keeps track of their customers online and instore shopping purchases and what websites their customers are looking at. They target which advertisements and coupons they want to send to their customers. Their ability to profile consumers led to a father getting angry because he didn’t know why his teenage daughter was getting ads for products for pregnant women. Little did he know that his daughter was pregnant and hadn’t told him yet, but Target knew through her cookies (Hill).
Current Data Collection in Companies
Some might say that it’s just consumers companies are targeting, but there are more than just consumers who are being watched. In recent years companies have started to track their assets even more closely. Amazon and Humanyze both collect data on their employees as they are at work. Amazon keeps track of how many items are being processed by each employee. Their RF scanners keep track of how fast they are working and if they don’t meet their quota they are reported by the system to their managers. Employees at Amazon report that their quotas are very high and if you don’t scan an item every 10 seconds you can’t hope to meet them (Skarda).
This system, while strenuous, makes sense for many people whereas Humanzye’s tracking might seem much more intrusive. They require all employees to keep a badge on them that listens and tracks movement. It knows where they are in relation to where they are supposed to be and keeps track of every conversation, including keeping track of how many conversations you have with someone of the opposite gender (Areheart). The company claims they are tracking the working efficiency of their employees. But at what point do you draw the line for worker’s privacy? This sort of tracking can become more prevalent in other areas as computers can automatically keep tabs on employees as they go about their day. What if Humanyze never told their employees that they were monitoring them with AI? These situations are feasible, especially considering that companies like Teramind, ActivTrak, Hubstaff and Workpuls track what employees type and periodically take pictures of their screen for a manager’s review (HR_Brew). If it’s a work provided device that you use, employers can have the software installed on your computer before they even hand it to you.
Medical Data Collection
Being monitored without permission is a scary violation of privacy and no one wants that to happen to them . Even when we are told our data will not be shared with others, it’s not always as cut and dry as one would think. In the insurance and medical world, a set of laws called HIPPA protect patient data so that someone can get medical aid and their condition won’t be shared without their permission (Medical). However, this only applies if the data being shared can be identified with the patient. Insurance companies have found that if they remove all identification from the documents they receive, they can then sell the health care status of their patients online for billions (Enriquez-Sarano). This data is used by big medical companies to find potential solutions to medical issues. But should insurance companies be able to sell information about you that you were originally told would be private?
The insurance companies don’t stop at just selling your medical history, they also try to know more about you. This extra data is called shadow medical records. In his article about shadow medical records data, Price points out “Fitness trackers, web searches, shopping histories, social media posts, and mobile personal health apps can reveal information about an individual’s health” and the insurance companies want to know all that they can. This has led one company, IQVIA, to compile a record of 400 million comprehensive anonymous patient records (Price). This isn’t a question of it happening to someone else; this is happening to all of us.
Current Policies in Place
There’s hope! Despite all the issues we’ve previously discussed, some government entities are trying to start regulating these problems. The European Union (EU) passed the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in 2018, which states that companies must collect all their data legally, meaning they must receive consent from every individual. It also prohibits all foreign entities from collecting data on citizens of the EU (Price). This has pushed global companies to shift how they collect data. Even though we don’t live in the EU, this restriction has helped to curb some techniques of tracking.
Following the example of the GDPR, California has passed a law called the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) which helps protect only the data for individuals in California. The CCPA requires companies to give notice and access requirements when a business is accessing data and allows for individuals to delete their record history or even opt out it being sold. The CCPA has some loopholes like if your data is being used for peer-reviewed research then they might not need to notify you. It could also be that if the research is in the public interest, then it doesn’t need to be disclosed (Price). Unfortunately, there is not a federal law in place currently to protect individuals not from California from these issues.
Possible Policy Changes
If we want a federal law, research needs to be conducted to prove the need. If the government does not believe it to be an economic asset to the individual, it’s unlikely that they will protect it (Harris). The federal government currently has laws that effect data privacy, but they are limited to very specific industries and are riddled with loopholes because it only protects the types of data that the government believes are important (Harris). If we want a program that will bring more protection to consumers, we will need to research the gaps in these laws and find the best way to help consumers. California, for example, has 100 data privacy laws. However, they are all limited to content, potential victims, and modality (Harris). These limitations focus on worst case scenarios and don’t address the original problem which is if the consumers should own the data or not.
A secondary issue is who would enforce the laws that are enacted. Currently it’s the role of the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) to enforce cyber security concerns. Their reach, however, is limited due to laws enacted in 1914 which were made to prevent certain companies from being investigated (Harris). The FTC has power over business customer relationships, but they have no control over third parties that collect data and never talk to a consumer. The power of the FTC seems to focus more on “after the fact fixes” to the problem instead of a preventative focus. I think it’s safe to question that if the FTC is currently capable of policing the laws that are currently in place, maybe a change in how the laws are enforced is in order as well. Research could be conducted to help find potential changes to old laws that prevent the FTC from doing what is needed.
One might wonder what the consequences would be if changes are made to the system we currently have. Google and Facebook can provide free services because they have the right to sell the data they collect from you. It might be impossible to create laws that protect individuals if we leave loopholes open for companies that we see as good. It could be that if laws were put into place, insurance companies would increase their rates because they have reduced revenues. More laws could just lead to hurting honest businesses that care about their customers while other businesses just see paying FTC fines a part of doing business (Harris).
Conclusion
Questions about who truly owns this data need to be resolved and laws need to be put into place to reflect that. We currently don’t have an answer to all the questions, but through more research into how other governments like the European Union handle data privacy, we believe that we can come up with a more stable solution . We also believe that research needs to be conducted in the realm of consumers’ wants to see what kind of solution would best fit the culture of our people. Through research of current practices, we can also find solutions that might be simpler than passing new laws by amending what laws we currently have and repurposing them.
Whether it’s web surfing, employee tracking, or medical world data— data privacy is an important topic that will not soon go away. Concerns about data privacy need to be addressed on a national level and through time and effort, solutions can be found to help enlighten the public about these issues and find ways to protect their futures. Data privacy is an issue that applies to everyone. We all live in the Information Age and every device we own is sending and reporting more and more information each day. We shouldn’t have to live in fear! Through our efforts we can find solutions that will help to protect interests of each individual and the community at large.
